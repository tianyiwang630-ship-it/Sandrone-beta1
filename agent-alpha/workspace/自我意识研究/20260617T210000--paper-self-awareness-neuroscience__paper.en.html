Self-Awareness Doesn't Live in a Brain Region — It Emerges from the Interaction of Six Processes

The cognitive neuroscience of self-awareness — Mograbi, Hall, Arantes & Huntley (2023/2024)
Authors: Daniel C. Mograbi (PUC-Rio / KCL), Simon Hall (Camden & Islington NHS), Beatriz Arantes (PUC-Rio), Jonathan Huntley (UCL / Exeter)
Journal: WIREs Cognitive Science, 2024, 15(2): e1670  |  Type: Advanced Review  |  DOI: 10.1002/wcs.1670

1. The Question

Ask yourself: "What is self-awareness?" Most people answer: "I know that I am me." You follow up: "Where does this happen in the brain?" The classic answer points to the prefrontal cortex — that most "human" layer of cortex. But one look at the clinical data and you feel uneasy: the self-awareness problems in schizophrenia are comprehensive — body sense, agency, metacognition, autobiographical memory all disrupted. In autism, interoceptive abnormalities and emotional regulation abnormalities coexist. In depression, it's not just lowered self-evaluation — autobiographical memory is collapsing too. In borderline personality disorder, emotional regulation completely breaks down. In Alzheimer's, autobiographical memory peels away piece by piece.

These disorders show no pattern of any single brain region monopolizing the damage. If PFC broken → self-awareness gone were true, we'd see a group of PFC lesion patients displaying uniform symptoms. Clinical reality is not like this.

So we may have been asking the wrong question. Don't ask "which brain region houses self-awareness." Ask "which processes, under what framework, interact to let self-awareness emerge." Mograbi's team wrote this paper to answer exactly this question.

2. The Six Self-Awareness Processes

The paper delineates six self-awareness processes, each with distinct cognitive objects and neural substrates. Key distinction: Bodily self-awareness (interoception → body ownership → agency, forming a nested hierarchy — interoceptive signals also feed into body ownership and agency) and higher-order self-awareness (metacognition, emotional regulation, autobiographical memory — parallel and interactive but influenced by implicit bottom-up signals).

Selection criteria for the six processes: clear neurobiological basis + evolutionary perspective (less complex versions exist in other species). The paper explicitly excludes self-reference, self-reflection, inner speech — they may be higher-order "outcomes" rather than "foundational processes." Inner speech may be important in higher-order self-awareness but is not necessary — beings without language (infants, animals, severe aphasia patients) can still have integrated self-awareness.

Dual Pathways of Agency

Renes et al. (2013)'s classic experiment demonstrated: schizophrenia patients show normal agency under explicit conditions (consciously controlling square position), but significant absence under 17ms subliminal priming in the implicit pathway. This shows agency has two paths — the implicit efference copy comparison circuit and the explicit conscious inference circuit — schizophrenia first breaks the implicit pathway, which is more fundamental than the explicit pathway.

Autobiographical Memory Continuum

Episodic memory ("that winter in sophomore year, my friend and I…") and personal semantics ("I studied at X University") are not binary opposites — most memories fall between the two poles. But different retrieval modes correspond to different neural substrates (Burianova & Grady, 2007), which is clinically significant: recurrent depression's autobiographical memory is overgeneralized — difficulty retrieving specific memories for positive and neutral stimuli, while excessively focused on negative memories. This mutually reinforces negative self-beliefs — autobiographical memory is both cause (overgeneralized memory cannot provide evidence for positive self-reference) and effect (negative self-concept filters memory retrieval).

3. Predictive Coding: A Unified Cognitive Architecture

The paper's core architectural choice is placing all six processes within Predictive Coding. The framework's roots are in Helmholtz's concept of "unconscious inference," mathematically formalized in Friston's free energy principle. But systematically applying PC to all six sub-processes of self-awareness — that is new.

PC's core logic for self-awareness: The brain continuously generates top-down predictions (priors) and compares them with bottom-up sensory information. Mismatch = prediction error. Minimizing prediction error has two directions: revise beliefs (update internal self-model) or change input (act on the world/body to change it). All six processes are doing the same thing — just with different input sources.

ISAM: The Integrated Self-Awareness Model

The paper's Figure 1 ISAM (Integrated Self-Awareness Model) has three core claims:

  1. Nested hierarchy of bodily self-awareness — interoceptive signals feed into body ownership, body ownership feeds into agency judgments. Forming a nested but not strictly hierarchical structure.
  2. Higher-order self-awareness is influenced by implicit signals but expressed as explicit narrative self-representations — metacognition / emotional regulation / autobiographical memory are not necessarily hierarchical but parallel and interactive.
  3. Bodily self and higher-order self interact at both the process level and the integrative level — ultimately giving rise to an integrated self-awareness experience. This is not the product of any single process, nor the activity of any single brain region.

Emergence Rather Than Entity

Key insight: Self-awareness is an emergent property — lower-level interactions produce higher-level properties that are irreducible to the simple sum of lower-level components. This means: (1) Self-awareness is not a localizable "thing" — it is a process, not an object; (2) As cognitive complexity decreases (decomposing downward), subjective experience disappears at some level of reduction (Dennett, 2014); (3) Self-awareness is a graded phenomenon, not binary — from implicit homeostatic monitoring all the way to recursive metacognition. The simplest organisms can self-monitor without higher-order self-awareness; evolution stacked low-level self-monitoring mechanisms into increasing complexity, eventually giving rise to human-level reflexive thought.

Metacognition, emotional regulation, and autobiographical memory have no strict dependency chain — you can have emotional regulation collapse with intact metacognition (certain OCD), maintain basic emotional regulation with severely damaged autobiographical memory (early Alzheimer's). But they are strongly coupled, with the cross-coupling matrix as follows:

4.3 Bidirectional Circulation Between Bodily and Higher-Order Layers

Bottom-up path (from viscera to narrative): Interoception → Emotional Regulation ("racing heart" → AI matches with conceptual knowledge → "anxiety") → Autobiographical Memory (anxiety experience encoded as autobiographical event) → Metacognition ("my judgment may be affected by emotion"). Neural pathway: Mid-posterior insula → AI → Amygdala → Hippocampus → PCC.

Top-down path (from narrative to viscera): Autobiographical Memory → Emotional Regulation → Interoception (PTSD traumatic memory → amygdala→insula pathway → interoceptive system in persistent hypervigilance). Neural pathway: Hippocampus → Amygdala → AI / Mid-posterior insula → Brainstem autonomic nuclei.

Three key insights: (1) Emotional regulation is the only process engaging all three networks — it simultaneously needs SN to detect salience, CEN to execute strategies, and DMN to provide self-narrative context, explaining why borderline personality disorder's emotional collapse is so devastating; (2) No process uses only one network — the bodily and higher-order layers are continuous, not separate; (3) SN's AI is the "master scheduler" of the entire six-process architecture — AI not only switches "external task vs. internal self" but also decides which process occupies the foreground at any moment of internal self-processing.

5. Clinical Validation: Different Disorders Strike Different Processes

Schizophrenia is the most comprehensive strike — abnormalities in nearly all six processes. This explains this disorder's distinctive subjective experience: "I am no longer the master of my thoughts and actions." The dorsal mid-insula is a transdiagnostic convergence point — interoceptive abnormalities across multiple psychiatric conditions share common neural signals here (Nord et al., 2021).

Clinical significance: Different damage patterns point to different treatment strategies. Top-down pattern → cognitive intervention; bottom-up pattern → pharmacological/physiological intervention; bidirectional loop → simultaneous intervention in both directions. Depression responds to CBT (changing negative beliefs → correcting interoceptive interpretation); AD can currently only slow bottom-up memory system decline; schizophrenia requires comprehensive approaches.

5.5 Emergence Dynamics: Seven Steps from Implicit Self-Monitoring to "I Am Me"

Previous sections covered the interaction architecture — who connects to whom. This section covers the emergence process along the timeline: starting from the lowest level of implicit self-monitoring, layer by layer upward, at which step "self" transitions from non-conscious to conscious. Mograbi repeatedly emphasizes self-awareness is an emergent property — lower-level interactions produce higher-level properties irreducible to the sum of lower-level components. This is not magic — it has steps.

Precision Weighting: What Determines How Far a Signal Can Travel

After walking through the seven steps, the core question is: what determines whether a prediction error is silently eliminated at Step 1, or propagates upward all the way to Step 6 to become part of "who I am"?

The answer lies in precision weighting — the PC framework's most critical but most easily overlooked parameter. Each level's prediction carries a precision estimate — the brain predicts not just "heart rate should be 70," but also "how confident am I about this heart rate prediction." Higher precision → prediction errors on that channel are less tolerated → more easily passed upward.

Precision is learnable — through repeated experience. If a person repeatedly experiences "racing heart → panic → sense of impending death," the insula's precision weight on interoceptive signals becomes higher and higher. Eventually, a tiny heart rate change generates a massive weighted prediction error → passes through all seven steps → encoded as "I nearly died again" → further pushes up the precision weight. This is anxiety's positive feedback dynamics.

Conversely, one mechanism of mindfulness meditation may be lowering the precision weight on interoceptive signals — training the insula to process interoception at lower precision → same bodily sensation produces smaller weighted error → less frequently triggers the catastrophic cascade.

Summary Logic of Emergence

Self-awareness is not the product of any single layer — it is the result of Steps 0→6 all running in parallel, each step passing weighted prediction errors upward. Step 0's homeostatic monitoring is the "material," Steps 1-2's predictive coding and salience detection are the "filtering," Steps 3-4's network switching and global integration are the "scene construction," Step 5's self-attribution is the "ownership," Step 6's narrative integration is the "cross-temporal stitching." Removing any single step won't make self-awareness disappear, but it will change its form — remove Step 6 (AD) → lose cross-temporal identity; remove Step 5 (schizophrenia) → experience fragmentation; remove Steps 2-3 (autism) → external social self damaged but narrative self can remain intact.

6. Future Directions: Eight Paths

The paper offers eight paths; I'll highlight the three most noteworthy:

④ Psilocybin as a self-awareness probe: Psilocybin induces "ego dissolution" — reduced self-reference, dissolution of self/world boundaries. This is a reversible, dose-dependent, temporary alteration of self-awareness. It provides a natural probe: if you temporarily disrupt the top-down priors (self-model) in predictive coding, how are the six processes each affected? This experiment is ethically feasible and directly tests ISAM's framework predictions.
⑥ Machine self-awareness: The paper proposes — if you install an inner speech system in a robot, what kind of self-awareness would it develop? This is both a philosophical experiment (testing whether inner speech is sufficient for self-awareness) and reciprocally tests whether human self-awareness truly requires narrative. If a robot has inner speech but no self-awareness, Morin (2018)'s inner speech theory cracks. If a robot has inner speech and genuinely manifests some form of self-awareness — then our definition of "who is a possessor of self-awareness" must fundamentally change.

7. Conclusion

Self-awareness is not the prefrontal cortex's broadcast station (you already know this from Deco 2021), nor is it the exclusive activity of any single brain region. Mograbi 2023/2024 completes the other half: Self-awareness is a graded phenomenon emerging from the dynamic interaction of six processes — interoception, body ownership, agency, metacognition, emotional regulation, and autobiographical memory — under the predictive coding framework. It is not a localizable "thing," not the activity of a single brain region, not a binary switch. The simplest organisms have implicit self-monitoring without higher-order self-awareness — evolution stacked low-level self-monitoring mechanisms into increasing complexity, ultimately giving rise in humans to recursive reflexive thought.

Bressler & Menon (2010) gave you three-network dynamics — DMN self-system, SN scheduling system, CEN task engine.
Deco et al. (2021) used information flow to show: the global workspace is not in the prefrontal cortex, but in the DMN's midline cortex — precuneus / PCC / cingulate isthmus is the task-invariant "inbox."
Mograbi et al. (2023/2024) tells you: this "inbox" receives letters from six different sources — body, action, cognition, emotion, memory — and each source's letter has a different format, but all go through the same predictive coding "verification." Self-awareness does not come from the inbox's location; it comes from the verification process.

Advisor Review

Imagination in topic selection: Excellent. There are too many reviews on self-awareness — but the vast majority either focus on a single process (e.g., interoception), a single disorder, or get lost in grand philosophical narratives of consciousness. Mograbi's topic choice is unique — six processes as the horizontal axis, predictive coding as the vertical axis, and clinical disorders as the validation grid. The result: this grid covers self from the somatic level (interoception) through the narrative level (autobiographical memory), explaining both schizophrenia's global collapse and OCD's single-process anomaly.

Architectural ambition: ISAM is a framework worth citing. An imperfect framework is far more useful than perfect scattered observations — it generates falsifiable predictions. Weakness: the selection criteria for the six processes lack transparency — why these six? Why not include self-reference processing and inner speech? The paper provides explanations for what was excluded (these may be higher-order "outcomes" rather than "foundations"), but the argument is slightly underdeveloped. Neural network detail is thin — the paper barely discusses the role of DMN/SN/CEN in the six processes.

Clinical coverage: Table 1 is the paper's most practically valuable contribution — a six-process × dozen-disorder cross-reference table. That schizophrenia hits all six processes is not accidental; that BPD hits emotional regulation alone is not accidental.

Limitations: (1) No direct cross-disorder comparative studies exist — a limitation of both the review and the field. (2) The PC framework has both breadth and cost — the breadth is that it can be applied to anything; the cost is that it may explain everything but precisely predict nothing. (3) Neural network detail is thin — the original paper barely discusses the three networks' (DMN/SN/CEN) role in self-awareness. This document has supplemented this analysis in §4 (Interaction Dynamics) and §5.5 (Emergence Dynamics), connecting Mograbi's six-process framework with Bressler & Menon (2010)'s three-network framework and Deco et al. (2021)'s global workspace relocation.

Verdict: This is a good roadmap, not a good terminal station. Its value is helping researchers see clearly: self-awareness is not a localizable brain region — it is a cluster of capacities distributed across six processes, unified by the predictive coding architecture. The next step requires empirical substantiation.

Inspiration

Transfer: Is there someone in your company with a "schizophrenia-type structure" — problems across all six self-awareness processes? This means the person lacks an integrated self-narrative → cannot find stable self-positioning amid chaos → decision drift, emotional volatility, behavioral inconsistency. This type of person doesn't need performance review feedback (metacognitive training) — they need comprehensive self-narrative reconstruction. Conversely, an "OCD-type structure" — problems in only one process (low confidence → repeated checking) — what you should do is targeted process intervention rather than whole-personality restructuring.

Mashup: The PC framework doesn't just explain self-awareness. A company is also a system that continuously generates "predictions" (budgets, strategy, expectations) and compares them with "inputs" (market signals, employee feedback). Prediction error = budget variance, market surprise → either revise the internal model (adjust strategy) or change the input (change market conditions). But the "metacognitive" equivalent — a system that understands how well calibrated its own predictions are — is entirely absent in most organizations.

Inversion: The paper implies bodily self-awareness is the nested foundation — interoception is the lowest layer. But the reverse also holds: higher-order self-awareness can top-down shape bodily self-awareness. PTSD patients — distorted autobiographical memory (higher-order) → body in persistent hypervigilance (lower-order interoceptive bias). If ISAM incorporated dynamic weighting — in which contexts top-down weighting is higher, in which contexts bottom-up is higher — its explanatory power would be even stronger. Additionally: if inner speech is not necessary for self-awareness, does a machine need "inner speech" to have self-awareness? Or is having the information-integration architecture of the six processes sufficient? This is an open question — but the paper's framework at least gives you an experimental approach that can proceed without first solving "can machines think?"
